The big picture

Why do governments support airports and air routes?

Because market forces alone do not always protect essential connectivity. Airlines naturally prefer profitable routes, while socially valuable but less profitable airports and routes can be neglected.

Step 1

Airlines follow profit, not always connectivity

Private airlines naturally focus on profitable routes. That can leave essential connectivity underserved, especially in remote or lower-demand regions.

Step 2

Why government intervention exists

Policy must balance two goals at once: commercial sustainability and wider social connectivity. That is why governments support airports and air routes.

Step 3

Ad-hoc selection is not enough

The key problem is not just whether to support connectivity, but how to select the right airports and routes. Decisions should move from ad-hoc judgment to rational, data-driven policy.

Step 4

Our model brings the pieces together

Our optimization framework combines spatial interdependency, socio-economic factors, network effects, budget constraints, and demand considerations.

Step 5

Efficient spending with social gains

The goal is not simply to spend more or support more airports. The goal is to spend smarter, improve connectivity, and maximize public benefit.

Takeaway In short, the question is not whether governments should support regional air connectivity. The real question is: are we supporting the right airports, on the right routes, for the right reasons?
What problem are we solving?

We want a systematic way to choose airports and routes for regional connectivity schemes, instead of relying on random or ad-hoc selection.

What makes this research different?

The framework combines network structure, demand, and policy constraints, so it captures practical aviation effects.

Why use memes?

Memes make the research easier to remember and help non-specialists understand the main idea quickly.

Final message

What should policymakers remember?

Public support is useful, but it should be guided by evidence, rational criteria, and a clear objective: improve connectivity while using public resources efficiently.
